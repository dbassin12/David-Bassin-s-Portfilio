A four-day research project
01 · The Project

What This Project Is

Jewish history is often taught through its endings. This project asks what existed before.

The Big Idea

You research a single Jewish community as it actually lived before the Holocaust: its synagogues, schools, leaders, language, food, and daily rhythms. The goal is to understand these communities as living, complex societies — not as victims of what came after.

Slide Rules

  • Max 5 lines of text per slide. If you have more, cut it.
  • Body text: 24pt minimum. No walls of text.
  • Don't read your slides. Slides are visual support — you speak from knowledge.
  • Consistent design. Same font, same colors, same layout. The Visual Specialist checks this.
  • At least 1 map and 1 photo. The Source Bank links to image archives.
  • Cite every fact with a footnote number (see below).
Citations

Cite Your Sources on Every Slide

Footnote numbers on slides → numbered entries in the bibliography. Every fact must trace back to a source.

How Footnotes Work

When you state a fact on a slide — a population number, a date, a quote, anything specific — put a small superscript number after it. That number matches a numbered entry on the Bibliography slide. The reader (or the teacher) can trace any claim back to its source.

Why: Uncited claims look made-up. Cited claims look like research. The rubric rewards "information clearly drawn from credible sources" — this is how you show that.

Example slide content

By 1939, Vilna was home to roughly 60,000 Jews — about 28% of the city's population.1

The community supported over 100 synagogues, the Strashun Library, and the YIVO Institute, founded in 1925.2

"Vilna was a city of books," wrote one observer, "where every street had a yeshiva and every yeshiva had a printing press."3

Slide 12: Bibliography
  1. 1. YIVO Encyclopedia, "Vilnius." yivoencyclopedia.org/article.aspx/Vilnius
  2. 2. Yad Vashem, "The Story of the Jewish Community of Vilna." yadvashem.org/vilna
  3. 3. Lucy Dawidowicz, From That Place and Time: A Memoir, 1938–1947. New York: Norton, 1989, p. 54.

Bibliography Format

Number every source. Each entry should let the reader find the source themselves. Use this format:

  • Web source: Site name, "Article title." URL
  • Book: Author, Book Title. Publisher, year, page number(s)
  • Encyclopedia article: Encyclopedia name, "Entry name." URL

Minimum: at least 6 sources across the group (most groups have 8–12). Each role contributes their own sources to the group bibliography.

Two common mistakes to avoid.

(1) Don't put the URL alone — include the site name and article title so the reader knows what they're looking at. (2) Don't cite the same Source Bank link twice as two different sources — if it's the same article, it's one entry.

Q&A Preparation

Prepare for the 5-Minute Q&A

After your presentation, the teacher asks 2–3 quick questions. They are not meant to trick you — they confirm you understand your research.

Example Questions

  • What was this community known for?
  • Why was it located where it was?
  • What institutions mattered most to Jewish life there?
  • Who was an important figure and why?
  • What surprised you about this community?
  • How was it different from other Jewish communities?

Best prep: Close your laptop. Without looking at slides or worksheet, can you summarize your community's history, daily life, and significance in three sentences? If yes, you are ready.

07 · AI Policy

Honest Rules for Using AI

AI is a tool. Use it. But the Q&A confirms you understand your work — and no tool can answer for you.

The Bottom Line

AI can help you present your work. It should not do your work. The Q&A is worth 30 points, and if you do not understand what is on your slides, it will be obvious in about 15 seconds of questioning.

Go Ahead

AI can help you...

  • Improve slide design and formatting
  • Explain a term or concept you don't know
  • Brainstorm research questions or organize notes
  • Find sources (then go read the actual source yourself)
  • Clean up grammar on text you already wrote
Do Your Own Thinking

AI should NOT...

  • Write your worksheet answers for you
  • Summarize sources instead of you reading them
  • Generate the historical content on your slides
  • Form your conclusions about the community
  • Answer your Q&A questions on the spot
Why this matters.

After your presentation, you answer questions in your own words, on the spot. That is 30 of your 100 points. Students who did their own research will handle this easily. Students who had AI do the thinking for them will not.
